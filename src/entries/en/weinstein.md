title: Harvey Weinstein — The Case That Lit the Spark
tag: Convicted · NY + CA
---
The 2017 reporting on Harvey Weinstein – decades of alleged sexual abuse by one of Hollywood's most powerful producers – brought the #MeToo movement to global prominence and triggered a wave of accountability across industries. Precision matters here: the movement was not created by Weinstein – activist Tarana Burke had founded "Me Too" back in 2006 to give voice above all to marginalized survivors of sexual violence; the 2017 reporting ignited it and made it visible worldwide. The legal record is layered and worth laying out precisely. In 2020, a New York jury convicted Weinstein of a criminal sexual act and third-degree rape; he was sentenced to 23 years. In 2022, a separate Los Angeles jury convicted him of rape and sexual assault; he was sentenced to 16 years – a conviction that still stands. In April 2024, New York's highest court overturned the 2020 conviction in a narrow 4–3 ruling, holding that the trial judge had wrongly admitted testimony about uncharged alleged acts. Manhattan prosecutors retried him, and in June 2025 a jury again convicted him of a criminal sexual act (acquitting on one count; a mistrial was declared on a third). Net status: Weinstein is a convicted sex offender in two states, with the California conviction never disturbed. The New York saga of reversal and reconviction is a reminder that even landmark cases turn on procedural rules – and that accountability is rarely a clean line.
