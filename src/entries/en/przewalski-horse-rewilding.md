---
title: The Last Wild Horse — Back From Extinct-In-The-Wild
tag: "Win · conservation"
---
The Przewalski's horse — the only truly wild horse species left on Earth, never domesticated — was declared extinct in the wild after the last sighting in 1969. Every survivor descended from a tiny captive founder population of roughly a dozen animals held in zoos. Then it was brought back. China launched its "Wild Horses Return Home" program in 1985; a peer-reviewed account documents 24 founder horses transferred in five groups from zoos in Germany, the United Kingdom and the United States between 1985 and 2005 to a breeding center in the semi-desert Junggar Basin in Xinjiang, with the first foal born in 1988. Through decades of careful studbook management (overseen internationally by the Prague Zoo since 1959) to fight the inbreeding that threatens such a narrow gene pool, plus monitoring and step-by-step rewilding, China's population surpassed 900 by 2025 — about a third of the global total. The IUCN had already downgraded the species globally from "Extinct in the Wild" to "Endangered" in 2011. Honest caveats keep this a real win rather than a tidy myth: conservationists note the Chinese herds still rely on winter supplementary feeding and water in places, so a fully self-sustaining wild population isn't yet secured, and inbreeding remains a long-term risk. But the arc is genuine — a species that vanished from the wild within living memory now numbers in the thousands worldwide, and an ongoing partnership spearheaded by the Prague Zoo, Berlin's Tierpark, and the UN Environment Programme began airlifting horses in 2024 to rewild Kazakhstan's Golden Steppe, where they'd been gone for two centuries. Proof that some losses can be reversed.
