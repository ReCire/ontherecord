While Palantir builds surveillance infrastructure for ICE and the Pentagon in the US, the company has been expanding aggressively into Europe since the pandemic — and meeting the stiffest resistance it has faced anywhere. This entry's highest evidentiary tier comes from Germany: in February 2023, the **Federal Constitutional Court** [ruling] held that police use of Palantir's automated data analysis, in the form then under review, was **unconstitutional**, violating the right to informational self-determination; the court struck down Hamburg's authorising law and gave Hesse a deadline to rewrite its provisions. Despite the ruling, the federal government under Friedrich Merz reportedly brought a nationwide Palantir acquisition back to the table in 2025 under the "Polizei 20/20" programme — the software remains in use by police in several states (including Bavaria, Hesse, North Rhine-Westphalia).

In the United Kingdom, the fight is over public procurement. An investigation by *The Nerve* (January 2026) counted at least 34 state contracts across at least ten departments and agencies, worth at least £670 million combined — including a £330 million NHS contract (2023) for the "Federated Data Platform," which brings sensitive patient health data together on a Palantir-run platform. In December 2025, the Ministry of Defence awarded a £240 million contract **with no competitive tender**. On 3 June 2026, a cross-party House of Commons committee called on the government to terminate the NHS contract because the company supports "highly controversial" policies and activities; the British Medical Association (over 200,000 doctors) urged members to refuse any use beyond direct care. Reporting by *Liberty Investigates* and Corporate Watch documented that police forces were advised to respond to queries about Palantir software used for covert purposes with "neither confirm nor deny" — despite a pledge of "maximum transparency by default" for AI use.

The resistance is having effect: in May 2026, London Mayor Sadiq Khan blocked a £50 million Metropolitan Police deal, citing the company's "values." Switzerland turned Palantir down after a seven-year lobbying effort — an internal Swiss Army report voiced fears that Palantir could pass confidential data to the CIA and NSA (which Palantir denies, calling the report inaccurate). Tier note: the German constitutional ruling is an adjudicated fact; the rest — the procurement criticism, the data-protection concerns, the rejections — is documented, ongoing contestation, not a finding of guilt against the company. The throughline: the same architecture that supports deportations and warfare in the US is being sold to health systems, police, and militaries in Europe — and courts, parliaments, and city governments are starting to stop it.
